Things to know

  • Payment on account: After your first Self Assessment, HMRC splits the bill into advance payments — 50% due 31 January and 50% due 31 July. Budget for this in year two.
  • PAYE estimate: Based on your tax code. If HMRC has adjusted it for benefits-in-kind or other income, your actual PAYE figure may differ.
  • Personal allowance taper: If your total income exceeds £100,000 your personal allowance reduces — the effective tax rate between £100k–£125,140 is 60%.
  • Scotland: Scottish taxpayers have different income tax rates — this calculator covers England, Wales & Northern Ireland only.
  • This is an estimate only. Always verify with HMRC or a qualified accountant.

What is a tax code?

Your tax code tells your employer how much of your salary is tax-free. You can find it on your payslip, P60, or in your HMRC Personal Tax Account.

1257LStandard — £12,570 personal allowance (most people)
1100LNumber × 10 = allowance (e.g. 1100L = £11,000)
BRNo allowance — all salary taxed at 20% basic rate
D0All salary taxed at 40% higher rate
D1All salary taxed at 45% additional rate
0TNo personal allowance, uses normal tax bands
NTNo tax deducted at all
K codesUnusual — means deductions exceed your allowances

If HMRC has adjusted your code (e.g. due to a company car or underpaid tax), the PAYE estimate will reflect that — which may reduce your Self Assessment bill.

The £1,000 trading allowance

The trading allowance lets you earn up to £1,000 from self-employment without paying any tax or needing to register with HMRC.

If your gross income is over £1,000, you can still choose to use the allowance — deducting a flat £1,000 instead of totting up your actual expenses.

Use trading allowance when...
  • Your actual expenses are less than £1,000
  • You have very few receipts or records
  • You want a simpler calculation
Use actual expenses when...
  • Your business costs exceed £1,000
  • You have significant equipment or travel costs
  • You work from home and claim a portion of bills

You cannot combine the trading allowance with actual expenses — it's one or the other.

What are allowable business expenses?

You can deduct costs that are wholly and exclusively for your business. Here are the most common ones:

Office & workspace Stationery, postage, printer ink, a portion of home broadband/phone if used for work
Home office A proportion of electricity, heating, and broadband — based on hours worked or rooms used. HMRC flat rate: £6/week (no receipts needed)
Travel Business mileage (45p/mile up to 10,000 miles, 25p after), train or bus fares. Not your regular commute
Equipment & tools Laptops, cameras, tools, phones — if used for work. If used for both, claim the business proportion
Software & subscriptions Business software, cloud storage, professional memberships, trade magazine subscriptions
Marketing Website costs, hosting, domain names, advertising, business cards, social media ads
Professional fees Accountant or bookkeeper fees, legal costs, professional indemnity insurance
Stock & materials Goods you buy to sell on, or raw materials used to make products
Bank charges Business bank account fees and transaction charges
You cannot deduct Personal food or entertainment, everyday clothing, your regular commute, or any personal expenses

Keep receipts for everything. HMRC can ask for evidence up to 6 years after the tax year.
